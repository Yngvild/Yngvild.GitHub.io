Demography and matrix models

2 Life tables

Learning goals

  • Explain the difference between a dynamic and static life table.

  • Perform simple life table calculations (e.g. net reproductive rate \(R_0\), generation time \(T_C\)).

  • Write down the Euler-Lotka equation for a given life table and explain how it can be used to find the long-term growth rate \(\lambda\).

  • Discuss the advantages and disadvantages of different fitness measures.


2.1 What is a life table?

A life table is a simple method to keep track of births and deaths of individuals across age. Life tables are used extensively in human demography, social sciences, and epidemiology, for instance to calculate the remaining life expectancy at each age and project future mortality rates. In life history theory, which focuses on a wide range of species and understanding of their life history strategies, life tables are used to calculate important parameters for ecological and evolutionary dynamics, such as the net reproductive rate \(R_0\) and the long-term population growth rate \(\lambda\). Life tables are closely connected to age-structured matrix models and life cycle graphs - these are all different ways to describe the life history of a given population. While life tables are useful for manual calculations, matrix models are the most efficient way to do life history calculations, and have several additional useful properties.


2.2 A basic life table

In the most basic form, the life table is a list of age-specific parameters of age-specific survival (survivorship \(l_x\) and annual survival probability \(p_x\)) and fecundity (\(m_x\)):


Age \(x\) Survival probability \(p_x\) Survivorship \(l_x\) Fecundity \(m_x\)
0 \(p_0\) \(l_0=1\) \(m_0\)
1 \(p_1\) \(l_1=p_0\) \(m_1\)
2 \(p_2\) \(l_2=l_1p_1\) \(m_2\)
\(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\) \(\vdots\)
k \(p_k=0\) \(l_k=l_{k-1}p_{k-1}\) \(m_k\)


No individual lives past the final age, so the annual survival probability in the final age class is \(p_k=0\). However, before they die, individuals in this final class can produce \(m_k\) offspring on their \(k\)’th birthday. The fecundity will also be zero for the first age classes, until the age of maturation.

For sexual species, life tables are often defined only for the female subpopulation, under the assumption that survival and reproduction of females are not influenced by the number of males. In that case, the male and female subpopulation will grow with the same rate, and it does not matter which of the subpopulations we use to describe population growth. If female reproduction or survival is limited by the number of males, more complex two-sex models are needed to describe the population growth (see e.g. Caswell 2001). In our case, where we assume that males do not limit population growth, the fecundity parameter \(m_x\) represents the number of female offspring, unless it is specifically mentioned that it includes both male and female offspring. In that case wee need to first find the number of female offspring, which is the relevant number for the life table, and take into account the offspring sex ratio. If the offspring sex ratio is 1:1, the number of female offspring is simply the number of offspring divided by 2.


2.3 Dynamic and static life tables

There are two main ways of collecting data and estimating the age-specific parameters of survival and reproduction in a population. With a dynamic life table, also known as a cohort life table, an entire cohort of individuals are followed from birth to death, and their mortality and fecundity is recorded at each age. This approach is often expensive and time-demanding, and in many cases it is not practically possible to get such data. Another kind of life table is the static life table, which is based on recording the survival and reproduction of individuals in each age class over just one time step. This approach is usually more feasible, and corresponds to taking a cross-section of the population at a given time. However, it requires that we are able to determine the age of individuals, which is difficult in many species.

Importantly, the static life table approach is based on the assumptions that 1) the population is stationary (constant or near constant population size over time), and 2) the population has a stable age structure (the proportions of individuals in each age class do not change over time). A dynamic and static life table for a given population will be the same if the age-specific parameters of survival and fecundity are constant over time.


2.4 Life table calculations

Several properties can be calculated from a life table. We will focus on two of them: The net reproductive rate \(R_0\), and the cohort generation time \(T_C\). The net reproductive rate represents the number of female offspring by which each female will be replaced, on average:

\[\begin{align} R_0=\sum_{x=0}^k l_xm_x. \tag{2.1} \end{align}\]

The product \(l_xm_x\) represents the number of offspring produced at age \(x\) given that the individual survives to that age. Thus, summing up this product for all ages we get the net reproductive rate.

The net reproductive rate is a measure of the population growth rate per generation. For instance, if \(R_0=1.1\) the population will grow by 10% each generation. Thus, \(R_0\) holds information on the population growth rate \(\lambda\), although the two are not the same (\(\lambda\) is per time step, while \(R_0\) is per generation). If \(R_0>1\), we know that the population is growing (\(\lambda>1\)), if \(R_0=1\) then \(\lambda=1\) and the population is stable, and if \(R_0<1\) then \(\lambda<1\), and the population is declining.

The generation time reflects the ‘pace of life’ of a species, and many biological properties (physiological rates, other life history parameters) scale with it. For age structured populations there are several ways to define generation time. We will use the socalled cohort generation time, which is the mean age at reproduction of all the \(R_0\) offspring an average individual produces:

\[\begin{align} T_C=\frac{1}{R_0}\sum_{x=0}^{k} xl_xm_x. \tag{2.2} \end{align}\]

The expression \(xl_xm_x/R_0\) represents the fraction of the total expected reproduction (\(R_0\)) that occurs at age \(x\). By summing this up for all ages (and moving 1/\(R_0\) outside the sum) we get the average age at which reproduction occurs. This is also a measure of the average time from birth of an individual until the birth of her offspring.

Both the net reproductive rate and the generation time can also be calculated directly from a projection matrix, as we will see later.


Since \(R_0\) is an estimate of the per generation growth rate and \(T_C\) an estimate of generation time, we can use them to estimate the population growth rate per time step, the intrinsic growth rate \(r\) (see section ?? on exponential growth):

\[\begin{align} r \approx \frac{\ln R_0}{T_C}. \tag{2.3} \end{align}\]

This approximation assumes that all offspring produced during the lifetime on average (\(R_0\)) are produced exactly at age \(T_C\), rather than at different ages. It can be a good starting point for more accurate calculations based on the Euler-Lotka equation, which is the topic of the next section.


2.5 The Euler-Lotka equation

The Euler-Lotka equation is an essential equation in life history theory, because it provides the link between the life history of the focal population, as described by the age-specific fecundity \(m_x\) and survivorship \(l_x\), and the mean fitness \(\lambda\), associated with that life history.

Deriving the Euler-Lotka equation is considered advanced level knowledge in this course, although it is not very difficult. It is most important to know what the equation looks like, and to understand the importance in linking the life history (\(l_x\) and \(m_x\)) to fitness (\(\lambda\)). To derive the Euler-Lotka equation, we first define the number of newborn individuals at time \(t\), which is the sum of the newborns produced at each age:

\[\begin{align} n_0(t)=\sum_{x=0}^{k}n_0(t-x)l_xm_x. \tag{2.4} \end{align}\]

Here, \(n_0(t-x)\) is the number of newborn \(x\) years ago (that are now aged \(x\) years). This number is multiplied by \(l(x)\) to obtain the number of them that are still alive at age \(x\), and then by \(m(x)\) to get the total number of offspring they produce at age \(x\). The sum is over all ages, to get the total number of offspring (left side of the equation). For the next step of the derivation, we first assume that the population, and therefore each age class, grows steadily and exponentially at rate \(\lambda\). Because the population grows exponentially, we can guess that the number of newborn will follow an equation on the form \(n_0(t)=Q\lambda^t\) (remember the equation for exponential growth), where \(Q\) is a constant. Inserting this for \(n_0(t)\) and \(n_0(t-x)\) in the equation above, we now get

\[\begin{align} Q\lambda^{t}=\sum_{x=0}^{k}Q\lambda^{t-x}l_xm_x. \tag{2.5} \end{align}\]

Finally, we divide each side by \(Q\lambda^t\) to get the Euler-Lotka equation (remember that we can write \(\lambda^{t-x}=\lambda^{t}\lambda^{-x}\)):

\[\begin{align} 1=\sum_{x=0}^{k}\lambda^{-x}l_xm_x. \tag{2.6} \end{align}\]

It is tempting to try to rearrange this equation to isolate \(\lambda\) on one side, to get a nice expression for \(\lambda\) as a function of cumulative survival and fecundity. Unfortunately, this is not possible! The equation can only be solved numerically, for instance by trial and error (change the value of \(\lambda\) in the expression until you find the one that leads to the sum being close to 1).


2.6 Fitness measures in life history theory

In the context of life history theory, fitness is often measured by the intrinsic growth rate \(r\) (\(=\ln\lambda\)) as defined by the Euler-Lotka equation, or by the dominant eigenvalue of the projection matrix.

Another fitness measure which is commonly used, is the lifetime reproductive success, \(R_0\) \(\eqref{R0}\). This measure does however not account for timing of life history events (at which ages reproduction happens, for instance), and thus misses an important aspect of the life history. Both \(r\) and \(R_0\) are based on the assumption of constant age-specific survival and fecundity in the life table, and neither is a good measure of fitness in the case of strong density- or frequency-dependence (because \(r\) and \(R_0\) are both based on average values that are not useful for understanding selection in such situations). Life history theory deals mostly with outcomes of evolutionary processes in the long run (i.e. evolution of entire life histories) and to a less extent the short-term dynamics of gene frequencies (population genetics). In cases of strong density or frequency dependence, and where the purpose is to estimate gene frequencies over time, other methods are better suited (e.g. adaptive dynamics frameworks). These are not considered here, but note that the measure of fitness will also generally be different in these frameworks.

Here, we follow the convention through most of the literature on life history evolution and quantitative genetics based on demographic models, and use \(\lambda\) as our main fitness measure (or the equivalent \(r=\ln\lambda\)).


2.7 A fictive bird example


Figure 2.1: A songbird.


As an example, consider a songbird that can live up to 3 years, then reproduces for the last time and dies before reaching age 4. The life table for the female population is given by


Age \(x\) Survival probability \(p_x\) Survivorship \(l_x\) Fecundity \(m_x\) \(l_xm_x\)
0 0.2 1 0 0
1 0.9 0.2 0 0
2 0.6 0.18 3 0.54
3 0 0.108 6 0.648


The final column of this life table lists the product of survivorship and fecundity, \(l_xm_x\), and represents the expected number of offspring that an age 0 individual will produce at each age through life. This product occurs in the calculations of \(R_0\), \(T_C\) and \(\lambda\), and it is therefore often useful to include a separate column for it. We will return to this bird example in the following sections.


2.7.1 Calculations ‘by hand’

From the bird life table, we see that the age at first reproduction is 2, and that although few individuals live to age 3, this is the age class of highest expected number of offspring. The lifetime reproductive success is in this case

\[ R_0=\sum_{x=0}^3 l_xm_x=0+0+0.54+0.648=1.19. \] Thus, each female is expected to be replaced by 1.188 female offspring on average. From this number we also know that the population is growing (\(\lambda>1\)), although we do not yet know the exact value of the population growth rate \(\lambda\).

Using the measure defined above, the cohort generation time for the songbird is given by

\[ T_C=\frac{1}{R_0}\sum_{x=0}^k xl_xm_x=\frac{1}{1.118} (0+0+2\cdot0.54+3\cdot0.648)=2.54. \] The estimate of \(\lambda\) based on \(R_0\) and \(T_C\) is given by \[ \lambda=e^r\approx e^{\ln R_0/T_C}=e^{\ln 1.118/2.54}\approx 1.05. \]

The exact value of \(\lambda\) is defined by the Euler-Lotka equation, which for this population is given by

\[ 1=\sum_{x=0}^{k}\lambda^{-x}l_xm_x=0.54\lambda^{-2}+0.648\lambda^{-3}. \] We can always solve this by trial and error, and a good starting value for \(\lambda\) is the estimate from \(R_0\) and \(T_C\), \(\lambda \approx1.049\). However, this is more easily done in R, as shown below.


2.7.2 Calculations in R

We can also create a life table and do the above calculations in R. First we define the vectors for age class, age-specific survival, and fecundity. Then we define the life table using a data frame.


x <- 0:3 #Age
k <- length(x) #Maximum age
px <- c(.2, .9, .6, 0) #Annual survival probability
lx <- c(1, cumprod(px)[-k]) #survivorship probability
mx <- c(0, 0, 3, 6) #Fecundity

songbird.lifetable <- data.frame("x" = x, "px"=px, "l_x"=lx, "mx"=mx, "lxmx"=lx*mx)


Calculating the net reproductive rate and cohort generation time is straightforward using the vectors (note that R automatically multiplies each element of the vectors first, before summing the products):

R0 <- sum(lx*mx) #Net reproductive rate
T_C <- sum(x*lx*mx)/R0 #Generation time

The calculated values are \(R_0=\) 1.19 and \(T_C=\) 2.55.

Solving the Euler-Lotka equation in R is a bit more tricky (and not something you are expected to memorize). One of several possible approaches is using the function uniroot(). This function can be applied to another function (here: the Euler-Lotka equation) where the focal variable is the first argument (here: \(\lambda\)). Uniroot searches for the solution (the value that makes the function equal to zero) within a predefined range. For this to work for the Euler-Lotka equation, we need to rearrange it to get an expression that equals zero (move the number 1 from right-hand side to left hand side), make sure \(\lambda\) is the first argument, and then apply the uniroot function:

EulerLotka <- function(lambda  ){
  sum(lambda^(-x)*lx*mx)-1
}
lambda.range <- c(.1,2)
lambda <- uniroot(EulerLotka, interval=lambda.range)$root


With this approach the estimated value is \(\lambda=\) 1.07.

You have now seen an example of how the Euler-Lotka equation can be solved in R, but this approach is not commonly used. It is much more convenient to find \(\lambda\) based on the projection matrix from a matrix model, which is the topic of section ??.


2.8 Semelparity and iteroparity

Some species, including most mammals, have multiple reproductive events during their lifetime. This is known as iteroparity, while semelparity applies to species that reproduce only once. This mode of reproduction is used by annual plants as well as some perennials, several invertebrates, many fish species, and a few mammals. In salmon, some species are iteroparous and others are semelparous. From a life history perspective it is interesting to know under which circumstances evolution of iteroparity is favored, and we can now apply the knowledge from general age structured models to investigate it.

Cole (1954) was among the authors interested in this and other life history questions. He is known for Cole’s paradox, which was only a small part of a larger article. Here, Cole compared two species: The first is semelparous and lives for only a year, after which it produces \(m_1\) offspring and died. The second species is iteroparous and lives forever (survival probability of 1 each year), producing \(m_2\) offspring each year. Now we can calculate the annual growth rate (fitness) \(\lambda\) for each species. Species 1 has a growth rate \(\lambda_1=m_1\) (since each individual leaves behind \(m_1\) individuals on average each year), while the second species has a growth rate \(\lambda_2=1+m_2\) (each individual leaves behind \(1+m_2\) individuals each year, i.e. itself plus offspring). What would it take for the semelparous species to have higher fitness than the iteroparous one?

\[\begin{align} \lambda_1 > \lambda_2\\ m_ 1> 1+m_2. \end{align}\]

In other words, as long as species 1 produces at least 1 more offspring per clutch than species 2, it would have the same or higher fitness! This is known as Cole’s paradox, as it seems unlikely especially since species 2 is immortal. This is also the clue to the solution of the paradox: The assumptions for each species are too unrealistic to apply to the real world. Once we take more realism into account, for instance drop immortality but instead have an annual survival probability \(p<1\) for species 2, and introduce a difference between adults and juveniles (where juveniles cannot reproduce), iteroparity becomes more favorable and it would cost more to switch to a semelparous life history.


2.9 The Lack clutch

The concept of the Lack clutch originates from ornithology, but applies generally to other organisms as well. It is named after the ornithologist David Lack, who suggested a pioneering baseline model for understanding life history trade-offs that affect clutch size.

The problem started from the observation, including brood manipulation studies, that many birds seem to lay fewer eggs per clutch than they could manage to raise to fledging (independence). This suggests there could be one or more trade-offs preventing them from laying larger clutches, and that the observed clutch size perhaps represents some fitness optimum for the current environment. Lack’s model assumes that this trade-off is with fledgling survival, and he also assumed this trade off was linear. In that case, the optimum clutch size, from the parent’s point of view, is the one that maximizes the number of offspring that survive to fledging. This clutch size is known as the Lack clutch. The offspring, of course, would prefer (from a fitness perspective) to have all parental effort to itself, and the optimum clutch size for the offspring is 1 (just itself). This is an example of a parent-offspring conflict (where offspring and parents do not ‘agree’, evolutionarily speaking, on what is the optimum). We discuss other examples in the lectures.

We can describe the Lack clutch model mathematically as follows. Let \(N_e\) be the number of eggs laid in a clutch, and let \(l_f(N_e)\) be the probability of survival from egg laying until fledging. It is a linear function of the number of eggs in the clutch:

\[\begin{align} l_f(N_e)=1-cN_e, \end{align}\] where \(c\) is the slope of the relationship. The number of survived fledglings is then given by

\[\begin{align} N_f(N_e)&=l_f(N_e)N_e\\ &=(1-cN_e)N_e\\ &=N_e-cN_e^2. \end{align}\]


This is a quadratic function, and we can find the maximum by taking the derivative and find the value of \(N_e\) (the Lack clutch \(N_e^*\)) where this is equal to zero:

\[\begin{align} \frac{dN_f(N_e)}{dN_e}&=1-2cN_e\\ 1-2cN_e^*&=0\\ N_e^*=\frac{1}{2c}. \end{align}\]

This tells us that the stronger the trade-off with offspring survival (size of the slope \(c\)), the lower the Lack clutch size. The Lack clutch is larger than one only for \(c<0.5\). The Lack clutch is based on a simple model, with some important assumptions that may not be true in many cases. For instance, the model considers only one (but important) trade-off, with offspring survival. However, other trade-offs could also affect the clutch size, for instance parental survival and future reproduction. Another important assumption is that the trade-off between offspring survival and clutch size is linear, but this is just a convenient assumption and in many cases the offspring survival may be a non-linear function of clutch size (e.g. due to parasitism affecting large clutches only). Furthermore, the model assumes a constant environment, and that parents will treat each offspring equally. In many species, for instance in many raptors, parents will favor the largest / oldest of the siblings, so that younger siblings only survive in very good conditions (or if the older ones die for some reason). In these cases, the younger offspring may represent an “insurance” for the parents, and effects like this are not captured in the model for the Lack clutch.
